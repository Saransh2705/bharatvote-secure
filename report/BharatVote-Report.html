Abstract

BharatVote is a secure, government-grade digital voting platform designed for the Indian election system. It enables citizens to register as voters with identity and facial verification, cast their vote securely in ongoing elections, and independently verify that their vote was recorded — while election officials and the public can view results in real time. The platform is built as a modern full-stack web application using Next.js 15 (React, TypeScript) on the front end and the Supabase platform — a managed PostgreSQL database with authentication, storage, and auto-generated APIs — on the back end, requiring no separately operated server.

The system models the full electoral domain — voters, elections, candidates, votes, polling booths, official documents, and announcements — in a normalized relational schema protected by Row Level Security. Reference data such as elections, candidates, and results is publicly readable, while all sensitive operations (voter registration and vote casting) are performed exclusively through server-side routes using a privileged service-role key, ensuring the browser never holds write authority. Each vote is encoded, assigned a unique confirmation identifier, constrained to one per voter per election, and acknowledged by an email confirmation.

This report documents the motivation, requirements analysis, system design, implementation, testing, and deployment of BharatVote. It demonstrates how a security-conscious, real-time civic application can be delivered by an individual developer using contemporary managed cloud services, and evaluates the resulting system against its functional and non-functional goals.

Chapter One

Introduction

Elections are the foundation of a democracy, and the integrity, accessibility, and transparency of the voting process are of paramount importance. This chapter introduces the BharatVote platform, the problem it addresses, the motivation behind it, its objectives, and the scope of the work.

1.1 Overview

India conducts the largest democratic exercise in the world, with an electorate of nearly a billion voters spread across a vast and diverse geography. While Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) have modernized in-person polling, the broader electoral experience — voter registration, verification of identity, discovery of polling booths, access to official documents, and timely publication of results — remains fragmented across many channels. BharatVote is a unified, secure digital voting platform that brings these functions together in a single, modern web application designed for the Indian election system.

Through BharatVote a citizen can register as a voter and verify their identity using their EPIC (voter ID) number and a facial capture step, browse active and upcoming elections, cast a secure vote for a candidate of their choice, and independently confirm that their vote was recorded — all from a browser. Election administrators operate through a multi-level administrative model, and the public can follow results in real time. The platform is built with Next.js 15 and TypeScript on the client, and the managed Supabase platform (PostgreSQL, authentication, storage, and serverless APIs) as its backend, requiring no separately operated server.

1.2 Problem Statement

Designing any voting system — digital or physical — is uniquely demanding because it must simultaneously satisfy properties that are often in tension:

  • Integrity. Each eligible voter must be able to cast at most one vote per election, and recorded votes must not be alterable.
  • Authentication. Only verified, eligible citizens should be able to vote, which requires reliable identity verification.
  • Confidentiality & verifiability. A vote must be private, yet the voter must be able to confirm that their vote was counted.
  • Transparency. Results must be publicly auditable and available in a timely manner.
  • Accessibility & trust. The system must be usable by ordinary citizens and must be resistant to tampering at every layer.

The problem this project addresses is to design and build a digital voting platform that embodies these properties in a coherent, working system, using an architecture that enforces security at the data layer rather than relying solely on application code — and to do so with modern managed infrastructure that an individual developer can operate.

Problem, in one sentenceHow can a secure, verifiable, real-time digital voting platform — with identity verification, one-vote-per-election integrity, and public results — be built for the Indian election system using a database-enforced security model and managed cloud services?

1.3 Motivation

The motivation for BharatVote arises from two observations. First, citizens increasingly expect government services to be as accessible and responsive as the consumer applications they use daily; a fragmented electoral experience is at odds with that expectation. Second, the maturity of Backend-as-a-Service platforms and modern React frameworks now makes it feasible for a small team — or an individual — to build data-intensive, security-sensitive applications that once required large engineering organizations.

Building BharatVote is therefore both an instructive engineering exercise and a demonstration of civic-technology potential. It requires a deep, applied engagement with relational data modeling, database-level access control, secure server-side API design, identity verification, and real-time data visualization — all within a domain where correctness and security genuinely matter. The project shows that contemporary tooling can bring ambitious e-governance applications within reach.

1.4 Objectives

  1. To model the electoral domain — voters, elections, candidates, votes, booths, documents, and announcements — as a normalized, secure relational schema.
  2. To implement voter registration with EPIC-based identity capture and a facial-verification step.
  3. To enable secure vote casting that enforces one vote per voter per election, encodes the vote, and issues a unique confirmation identifier.
  4. To provide independent vote verification via the confirmation identifier.
  5. To publish election results and analytics in real time.
  6. To enforce data privacy and integrity at the database layer using Row Level Security, with all writes performed through privileged server-side routes.
  7. To deliver the system as a self-contained, deployable full-stack application on managed cloud infrastructure.

1.5 Scope of the Project

BharatVote is developed as a functional, demonstrable platform covering the complete voter journey — registration, verification, election discovery, voting, confirmation, and results — together with supporting modules for polling-booth information, official documents, announcements, and a multi-level administrative view. Identity checks that depend on external government infrastructure (such as live Aadhaar authentication and biometric face-matching services) are represented within the application through their integration points and simulated for demonstration, since access to those national systems is outside the remit of an academic project. Similarly, production-grade cryptographic vote encryption and formal end-to-end verifiable protocols are identified as future enhancements. Within these bounds, the project delivers a working, security-conscious system backed by a live cloud database.

1.6 Significance of the Study

The significance of BharatVote lies in demonstrating that the twin demands of a voting system — rigorous security and broad accessibility — can be reconciled in a modern web application without the heavy, self-operated infrastructure historically required. By enforcing integrity and privacy at the database layer rather than only in application code, the project establishes a reproducible pattern that other civic and security-sensitive applications can adopt. For the student, it consolidates learning across databases, security, web engineering, and system design into a single coherent artefact of professional relevance; for the institution and its industry partner, it exemplifies the outcome-oriented, industry-aligned education that the mentorship model seeks to foster.

The expected outcomes were a working, verifiable voting platform backed by a live database; a security model that provably prevents unauthorized reads and double-voting; and a body of documentation — this report and an accompanying presentation — communicating the design and its rationale to both technical and non-technical audiences. Each of these outcomes was achieved, as the following chapters evidence.

Chapter Two

Literature Review & Existing Systems

A responsible design begins with an understanding of how voting has been conducted and computerized elsewhere. This chapter surveys the principal categories of voting systems, examines notable digital-voting deployments, and reviews the technical approaches on which a secure, real-time platform must be founded.

2.1 Categories of Voting Systems

2.1.1 Paper Ballots

The traditional paper ballot is simple, transparent, and requires no technology, which underpins public trust in it. However, it is labour-intensive to administer and count, slow to produce results, and susceptible to invalidated ballots and logistical delays. Its strengths — a durable physical audit trail — inform the design goal of an immutable, verifiable record in digital systems.

2.1.2 Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs)

India’s EVMs record votes electronically at the polling station and have dramatically accelerated counting while reducing invalid votes. The later addition of the Voter-Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) added an independent paper record, reflecting a key principle: a voter should be able to verify their vote. EVMs are, however, standalone devices used only for in-person polling and do not address registration, verification, or remote participation.

2.1.3 Internet / Remote E-Voting

Several jurisdictions have piloted internet voting. Estonia’s i-Voting is the most established, allowing citizens to vote online using a national digital identity (ID card) with strong cryptography and a re-voting mechanism to resist coercion. Pilots such as Voatz (mobile, biometric-authenticated voting) in the United States explored smartphone-based voting but drew scrutiny over security and transparency. These systems demonstrate both the promise of remote participation and the exacting security bar it demands.

2.1.4 Blockchain-Based Voting

Research and pilots have explored distributed ledgers to provide tamper-evidence and public auditability. While blockchains offer immutability, studies note that they do not by themselves solve voter authentication, ballot secrecy, or client-device security, and they introduce complexity. The relevant lesson for BharatVote is the value of an immutable, auditable record — which this project achieves through an append-only votes table with database-enforced constraints rather than a distributed ledger.

2.3 Enabling Technical Approaches

2.3.1 Database-Enforced Access Control

PostgreSQL’s Row Level Security (RLS) allows access rules to be attached to the data itself, so that a query can only return or modify rows permitted by policy — independent of application code. For a voting system this is decisive: public reference data (elections, candidates, results) can be safely exposed for reading, while writes and sensitive tables are reachable only through privileged server-side code. BharatVote adopts this model.

2.3.2 Server-Side Privilege Separation

Modern full-stack frameworks such as Next.js allow trusted logic to run in server-side route handlers that never ship to the browser. By performing all writes (registration, vote casting) with a privileged service-role key on the server, while the browser holds only a restricted anonymous key, the platform ensures that no client can bypass the intended rules.

2.3.3 Real-Time Data & Visualization

A managed data platform with instantly queryable APIs, combined with client-side charting (bar and pie visualizations), enables results to be presented live as votes accrue, without a bespoke analytics pipeline.

2.4 Security Requirements of Electronic Voting

The study of prior systems distils a set of security properties that any credible electronic voting system must satisfy, and which directly shaped BharatVote’s design:

  • Eligibility. Only registered, verified citizens may cast a vote.
  • Uniqueness. Each eligible voter may vote at most once per election.
  • Integrity. A recorded vote cannot be altered or deleted, and the tally reflects exactly the votes cast.
  • Ballot secrecy. A voter’s specific choice is not exposed to other parties.
  • Verifiability. A voter can independently confirm that their vote was recorded.
  • Availability. The system remains usable throughout the polling window.

BharatVote addresses eligibility and uniqueness through registration and a database uniqueness constraint; integrity through an append-only ballot log; secrecy by confining all access to the votes table to trusted server code; verifiability through confirmation identifiers; and availability by building on a managed, horizontally-scaled platform. Coercion-resistance and formal end-to-end cryptographic verifiability — the most advanced properties, exemplified by Estonia’s re-voting mechanism — are identified as directions for future work rather than claimed by the present system.

2.5 Web-Application Security Foundations

Beyond voting-specific properties, the platform rests on established web-security foundations. All communication occurs over TLS; secrets are separated into public values safe for the browser and privileged values confined to the server; the principle of least privilege governs the two database clients; and defense in depth is achieved by enforcing access rules both in server code and, independently, in database policies. Consequently a failure or manipulation in any single layer does not by itself compromise the system — a design stance especially important for an application in the electoral domain.

2.6 Summary

The survey confirms that a credible digital voting platform must offer verified identity, one-vote integrity, an immutable and verifiable record, and timely results — and that database-enforced access control with server-side privilege separation is an appropriate foundation. BharatVote’s contribution is to combine these into a single, coherent, deployable application. The next chapter translates these observations into concrete requirements.

Chapter Four

System Design

Design translates the requirements into a concrete structure. This chapter presents the architecture, the flow of data, the relational data model, the security design, and the user-interface design of BharatVote.

4.1 Design Principles

The system is designed around four principles. First, the database is the source of truth: voters, elections, candidates, and votes all live in PostgreSQL. Second, security is enforced where the data lives, through Row Level Security and privilege separation. Third, trusted logic runs on the server: registration and vote casting execute in server-side routes with a privileged key, never in the browser. Fourth, the votes record is append-only, giving an immutable, auditable log constrained to one vote per voter per election.

4.2 High-Level Architecture

BharatVote is a full-stack Next.js application with three logical layers: the browser client (React pages), the Next.js server routes (trusted API), and the Supabase backend (PostgreSQL, RLS, and auto-generated data APIs), with an email service for confirmations. 4.4 Database Design

The schema is normalized and consists of seven tables. Figure 4.3 shows the entity-relationship model.

4.4.1 Table Descriptions

  • users — registered voters keyed by a generated id, storing EPIC (voter_id), name, state, facial data, and role.
  • elections — elections with a type, state, dates, and a status (active/upcoming/completed).
  • candidates — candidates linked to an election, with party, symbol, and a running vote tally.
  • votes — the append-only ballot log; unique per (election, voter), carrying the encoded vote and a confirmation id.
  • booths — polling-booth directory with geolocation and officer.
  • documents / announcements — official notices and publications.

4.5 Security Design

Row Level Security is enabled on every table. Public reference tables — elections, candidates, booths, documents, announcements — carry a read-only policy so the browser’s anonymous key can display them. The sensitive tables — users and votes — have no anonymous policy at all; they are reachable only by the server-side service-role key, which bypasses RLS. Consequently, registration and voting can occur only through the trusted server routes, and no client can read another voter’s record or cast a vote on their behalf.

4.7 User-Interface Design

The interface adopts a government-portal visual language — an Indian tricolor accent palette, clear typography, and card-based layouts — implemented with Tailwind CSS and the shadcn/ui component library, with subtle Framer Motion transitions. Principal screens are the home page, voter registration (form → facial capture → confirmation), elections browser, voting flow (select → confirm → done), live results with charts, vote verification, booth locator, documents, and the administrative dashboards.

4.8 Component Architecture

On the client, the interface is decomposed into reusable components. A shared Layout provides the header, navigation, and footer; presentational components such as CandidateCard and ElectionCard render domain objects consistently across the voting, results, and election screens; and a library of primitives — buttons, inputs, selects, and dialogs — ensures visual and behavioural uniformity. Pages compose these components and own the data-fetching and flow state, which keeps the presentational components stateless and reusable and localizes complexity to the screens that need it.

4.9 State Management & Error Handling

Screen state is managed locally with React hooks — the current step of a multi-step flow, the selected election or candidate, and loading and error flags — which is sufficient because the application’s state is largely page-scoped. Data is fetched when a screen mounts and held in component state. Every network interaction is wrapped so that failures surface as clear, user-facing messages rather than silent errors: a rejected vote returns the server’s reason (for example, that the voter has already voted in the election), and auxiliary failures such as email dispatch are non-blocking so they never prevent the core action from completing.

4.10 API Design Conventions

The server routes follow consistent conventions: they validate required inputs and return a 400 response when any are missing; they perform their work with the appropriate database client — the anonymous client for public reads, the service-role client for privileged writes; and they return a uniform JSON envelope carrying either a data payload with a success flag or an error message with an appropriate HTTP status. Read endpoints accept optional query parameters, such as an election status or identifier, to filter their results. This uniformity makes the client code predictable and the API straightforward to reason about and extend.

4.11 Summary

The design establishes a three-layer, database-centric architecture in which integrity and privacy are enforced by Row Level Security and server-side privilege separation, the ballot log is append-only and one-per-voter, and results are derived from public reference data. 5.4 Data & Security Layer

All database access goes through lib/supabase.ts, which exposes two clients: a browser-safe anon client for reading public data, and a factory for a privileged service-role client used only on the server.

This concluding chapter summarizes what the project accomplished, reflects on its limitations, and outlines directions for future work.

9.1 Conclusion

BharatVote set out to build a secure, verifiable, real-time digital voting platform for the Indian election system using a database-enforced security model and managed cloud services — and, demonstrated by a working application backed by a live database, it succeeded. By combining Next.js server routes with the Supabase platform, the project unifies the previously fragmented electoral experience — registration, identity verification, election discovery, voting, vote verification, and results — into a single, coherent product.

The work yielded a normalized seven-table model of the electoral domain; a security model in which Row Level Security and server-side privilege separation guarantee that only trusted code can register voters or cast ballots, and that no client can read voter identities or votes; an append-only, one-per-voter ballot log that makes double-voting impossible by construction; and a real-time results experience. Beyond the artifact, the project was an applied study of secure system design, relational modeling, database access control, and modern full-stack engineering.

  • Vote encoding is a demonstrative encoding rather than a formal, end-to-end verifiable cryptographic scheme.
  • The voter session is a lightweight client session suited to demonstration rather than a full federated identity system.

  const encryptedVote = Buffer.from(JSON.stringify({ candidate_id,
    timestamp: new Date().toISOString() })).toString('base64');

    candidate_id, encrypted_vote: encryptedVote, confirmation_id: confirmationId }])
    .select().single();
  if (error)
    return NextResponse.json({ error: 'Failed to record vote' }, { status: 500 });

  const { data: cand } = await admin.from('candidates').select('votes').eq('id', candidate_id).single();
  await admin.from('candidates').update({ votes: (cand?.votes ?? 0) + 1 }).eq('id', candidate_id);

  // best-effort confirmation email (non-blocking)
  try {
    const { data: user } = await admin.from('users').select('email, full_name').eq('id', voter_id).single();

Risk Analysis & Security Considerations

A voting platform must be examined not only for what it does but for how it could fail or be attacked. This appendix records the principal risks considered during development and the mitigations adopted or planned.

J.1 Risk Register

Table J.1 — Risks and mitigations.

Double votingIntegrity — a voter counts twiceDatabase unique(election, voter) constraint plus a server-side pre-check.

J.2 Defense in Depth

The platform’s security does not rest on any single control. Even if the client application contained a defect, the database policies would still deny access to sensitive data; even if a server route omitted a check, the uniqueness constraint would still prevent double-voting; and even if a request were intercepted, transport encryption would protect its contents. This layering — application logic, server-side privilege separation, database policies, and transport security — is the essence of the defense-in-depth posture and is what makes the platform’s guarantees robust.

J.3 Residual Risks & Future Hardening

Certain risks are acknowledged as residual within the project’s scope and are addressed by the future work of Chapter 9. Strong identity assurance requires integration with national identity and biometric services; formal ballot-secrecy and coercion-resistance guarantees require an end-to-end verifiable cryptographic protocol; and a full audit capability would add tamper-evident logging. Documenting these residual risks explicitly is itself a security practice, ensuring that the platform’s current guarantees are neither overstated nor mistaken for those of a fully hardened national system.

L.2 Ethical Considerations

Because voting is a matter of public trust, the project was approached with corresponding care. The platform is presented explicitly as an academic demonstration: its identity-verification and vote-encoding mechanisms are documented honestly as demonstrative rather than production-grade, and the residual risks are recorded in Appendix J so that the system’s guarantees are neither overstated nor mistaken for those of a certified national voting system. No real voter data was used; all data in the platform is synthetic reference data seeded for demonstration. The design nonetheless takes the privacy and integrity of the (hypothetical) voter seriously at every layer, reflecting the ethical obligation that any electoral technology carries.

L.3 Data Handling

The platform stores only the minimum data necessary for its function, keeps voter records and ballots inaccessible to unprivileged clients, and separates public reference information from sensitive information at the database level. Secrets are never committed to version control. These practices reflect the principles of data minimization and least privilege that should govern any application handling personal or sensitive information.
